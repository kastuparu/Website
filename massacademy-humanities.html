Humanities and French

Humanities is a combination of a high school English and history class, taught by Ms. Small. For homework, we read short stories or selections from books and the following day had discussions about themes from the reading or ideas we found interesting. These discussions were my favorite part of the class because I got to share my thoughts with my classmates and to hear and respond to theirs as well. Learning about other perspectives from my classmates gave me much more insight on the topics we were learning about. In humanities, we also work on improved our writing skills. We had weekly journals and often wrote responses or essays. To the right is an image of me at Walden Pond, the location of Henry David Thoreau's Walden, a book our class read. Mrs. Wildfong’s language classes at Mass Academy, which are Spanish and French, are immersion-based. At my sending school, I took Latin for four years in middle and high school. At Mass Academy, I studied French. I found the transition between languages natural because it is a Romance language (I had already spoken Romanian at home and knew thousands of Latin words). In class, we focused on speaking French, practicing pronunciation and fluidity. Homework consisted of reading or writing in French. We also had a vocabulary and listening journal, in which we kept track of a few French vocabulary words that we learned each day and wrote about French movies, songs, or podcasts that we listened to twice a week. Ray Bradbury Essay

The first one was written in September and compared two of Ray Bradbury’s short stories, “August 2026: There Will Come Soft Rains” and “The Pedestrian,” written in 1950. They both dealt with the future of humanity being overcome by technology.

Rhetorical Analysis Essay

My next essay was about comparing the effectiveness of two pieces that argue for animal rights: “Babycakes” by Neil Gaiman and a BBC article titled “Animal Rights.” “Babycakes” is a satirical piece proposing that when the world runs out of animals, babies should be used in their place. “Animal Rights” takes an approach appealing more to logos and ethos because it presents logical arguments for supporting animal rights while quoting many intellectuals.

Science Fiction Essay

The final topic we covered in humanities class was science fiction. My essay discussed how early-mid 20th century sci-fi warned people of a future filled with the present-day wars through fear. I utilized several short stories we read in class, such as Alfred Bester's "Adam and No Eve," "Second Variety" by Philip K. Dick, and an adaptation of "The War of Worlds" read by Orson Welles.
